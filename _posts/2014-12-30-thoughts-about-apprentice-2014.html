Spoiler alert! Here are some very quick thoughts on The Apprentice 2014's final (I finally watched it!) and how I'd completely turn the sale of tights on its head.

At the end of the final, Lord Sugar says he's taking a "gamble" in picking Mark. As a product-oriented man, that may be true, but as a businessman it's an absolute no brainer. Mark can scale infinitely on a tried-and-tested business plan. It's kinda boring, but you can never blame a businessman for going for the safe option, especially with lots of money on the line.

Going into the final, I thought Bianca had it in the bag - her product met the needs of huge numbers of women in the UK (and beyond), it's a unique proposition - and it's innovative.

But she scuppered herself in pricing the product far beyond what any sane person would spend - £24 for one pair of tights. Even the final drop to £20 wouldn't see many women rushing to the shops to buy one. And that's kinda okay, because Bianca was angling for the premium market - but that's not what the product represents. It's a product for every woman.

Mark's winning words were "Absolute Customer Satisfaction", and Bianca should have taken a leaf out of his book - absolute customer satisfaction, and a pair of True Skin tights in every home. Price it appropriately so anyone could afford a pair and you'd have the audience of the pitch in united approval.

The pricing would be crucial - if people say they would pay £6 for a pair of tights, then sell it at £8. Bianca was correct in saying that her tights were a unique proposition, so the price can reflect that. At any rate, tights break all the time, so make a good product at a reasonable price and people won't think twice about giving you more money.

(Turns out Bianca actually plans to sell it at £8 after all. I'm a good guesser!)

In the final, Mark had an immediate advantage - no physical product to sell. This advantage, of course, exists in the real world and it's one of the reasons why digital is so empowering for a business anywhere in the world. Across 15 shades, 4 sizes, and 2 materials, the sheer vastness of Bianca's product range is enough to give pause to any shop owner.

But we saw how easy it was to make tights when the team tinted a few pairs. While I have no idea if that's how you'd make them en masse, it offers an avenue to a truly unique business. This is 2014, and the internet offers a whole new way of looking at how you make sales.

The tights themselves are a disruptive proposition - they bring new choices for women of all skin tones. So let's disrupt how you'd sell them. First of all, work out a way to bring that magical skin shading detection device to any shop in the country. A customer scans their skin colour. You make a pair of tights just for that skin tone. Then you deliver right to their door.

Remember their order, so they can quickly re-order another pair when they need to. Price it right, so they will be happy to make you their only choice for tights. Make it effortless to order for the first time or the hundredth. Deliver quickly so customers know they'll have tights in time for an upcoming event.

There is your business - not 15 shades, but infinite shades. At the right price, why would any woman ever bother to buy any other brand compared to their own unique tights?

It's a license to print money. At least, I think so.

(I know nothing about tights, but I like to think I know about the internet.)
